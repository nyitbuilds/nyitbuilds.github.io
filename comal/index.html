Comal

Every kitchen, in its place.

The software, and later the small machine, that kept independent family restaurants alive when the chains had every other advantage. This is its story, written forward from the day it did not yet exist.

Daniela Reyes & Suresh Iyer Co-founders · met at NYIT, 2026

A company is not a list of steps. It is a string of decisions made with too little information and too much at stake. Here are the seven that built Comal.

2026 · YEAR ZERO

The napkin.

Daniela kept the books at her family's restaurant, so she knew the number that scared her: a third of every delivery went in the trash before anyone ordered it. One slow week the waste nearly cost the staff a paycheck.

At NYIT she met Suresh, who built demand models for the fun of it. They sketched it on a napkin. Software that tells a small kitchen what to buy and what to prep, so it stops bleeding cash it never had.

The call

Whose problem, exactly? Not "restaurants." The independent owner with no buyer, no analyst, and no margin for a bad guess.

2027 · YEAR ONE

Customer number one.

Their first customer was the restaurant that started it. Comal told the kitchen what the weekend would need, and the waste line fell by half. The place survived its worst winter in a decade.

They charged two hundred dollars a month and signed three more restaurants on Long Island. It was real revenue, small and terrifying and theirs.

The call

Charge from day one, or grow free and hope to charge later? They charged. A thing nobody pays for is a hobby, not a company.

2028 · YEAR TWO

Two more hands.

Twelve restaurants. More than two college students could answer the phone for. They hired their first two employees and learned that a founder's hardest spreadsheet is the one with people's names on it.

They gave Comal a face: the logo, the voice, the promise to the owner who could not afford to be sold a gimmick.

2029 · YEAR THREE

The year it almost ended.

A venture-backed company bundled a free forecasting tool into the biggest restaurant point-of-sale system in the country. Overnight, Comal's one feature was free everywhere. Customers left. The savings ran out. One founder wanted to sell for parts. The other wanted to fight.

Daniela went back to the kitchens and asked what actually kept owners up at night. It was not forecasting. It was that they could not find or afford a prep cook. So Comal bet everything it had left on a small countertop machine that did the prep, the one thing a free download could never copy.

The call

Sell cheap and walk away whole, or spend the last of the runway on a machine they had never built? They built the machine.

Stage · Competition, near-death, the pivot
2030 · YEAR FOUR

The bet pays.

A diner an hour from closing stayed open because one machine did the work of the prep cook it could never hire. Then another. The pivot was not a slogan; it was a kitchen that kept its lights on.

For the first time, investors who had passed called back. Comal raised a real round, not to look successful, but to build machines faster than the orders were coming in.

The call

Take the money and answer to it, or stay small and stay pure? They took it, on the condition the mission came in writing.

2031 · YEAR FIVE

The underdog's company.

Hundreds of independent kitchens across the Northeast ran on Comal. The trade press called it the company keeping family restaurants alive against the chains. Daniela's own family restaurant was still customer number one, and still got a call on her cell when something broke.

Stage · Growth and market position
2032 · YEAR SIX

The offer.

The acquirer was a national food-tech giant, one of the same kind that had once tried to bury them with a free tool. They wanted Comal. The number was life-changing. The fear was simple: would the new owner keep serving the small kitchens, or quietly walk Comal upmarket toward the chains that could pay more?

The call

What is a company for? If Comal existed to save the independents, then the terms of the sale mattered more than the size of it.

2033 · YEAR SEVEN

They sold it right.

They sold, on one condition written into the deal: Comal would keep serving independent kitchens, by name, for years to come. They were twenty-four. They walked away having protected the thing they built, not just cashed it.

And the money was never the point. It was the runway for the futures they had each written on their first day at NYIT, seven years earlier.

Stage · The exit and what it was all for

What the exit bought

The same two futures they wrote on day one.

In the morning they each built a personal site and imagined themselves ten years on. Comal is how those two pictures come true. The company was the launchpad, not the destination.
